The StarTrack Project aims to bring STEM disciplines
into highschool classrooms within LAUSD.

Our goal is to engage students in truly hands-on STEM projects
and foster strong scientific and critical thinking skills.

In the first module we launched, every single student had the opportunity to build a barn door startracker assembly - a device used by astrophotographers to compensate for Earth's rotation and obtain stunning images of celestial bodies. Along the way we taught them fundemental concepts in astronomy, engineering, and computer science.

Module 1:
The Barn Door Star Tracker

The barndoor star tracker is a simplified equitorial mount, a $1000+ piece of equipment used to keep a camera or telescope continuously pointed at constellations, planets, nebulas, or other objects in the night sky. The "barndoor" rotates open, compensating for the rotation of the earth. It presents a solvable challenge to students, and delivers the satisfaction of producing both a device and stunning photos.

Astronomy:
Planetary Motion

We need a star tracker to perform astrophotography because of the rotation of the earth. How fast is the earth spining? What other movement is present in our Solar System? In our Galaxy? What kind of orbits and trajectories do celestial bodies follow and why?

Engineering:
Some Assembly Required

We let the students perform all of the assembly themselves. We teach them about the components and explain the factors that impact how an engineer chooses their hardware. When should you use wood, metal, or plastic? What governing equations are at play, and how does that affect our design?

Computer Science:
The Arduino Platform

We need ways to control the speed the barndoor opens at. Arduino is an open-source hardware and software platform that is widely used to teach and learn the basics of microcontrollers, and is widely used by the prototyping community. What are the basic building blocks of code? How is it being used elsewhere in our day-to-day lives?
